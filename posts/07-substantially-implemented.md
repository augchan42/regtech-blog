# What "Substantially Implemented" Means

*The art and anxiety of partial compliance*

---

Every compliance professional has been there: the compliance date is approaching, your team is working overtime, and you know you're not 100% ready. Can you ship what you have? Will regulators accept "substantially implemented"?

The honest answer: it depends. This post explains how compliance teams and regulators think about partial compliance.

---

## The Legal Framework

U.S. administrative law doesn't typically require **perfect** compliance — it requires **substantial** compliance. The standard is:
- Are you doing what the rule requires?
- Are you close enough that the gap doesn't matter?

This comes from administrative law principles, not from the rules themselves. Most regulations don't define "substantially implemented" — they assume you either comply or you don't.

*Source: Marquette University Law Library, "Federal Regulations"[^1]*

---

## How Regulators Think About Compliance

### The Spirit vs. The Letter

Regulators distinguish between:
- **Technical compliance:** Doing exactly what the rule says, even if the outcome is wrong
- **Substantive compliance:** Achieving the rule's purpose, even if the method differs

A regulator who sees a company doing the right thing imperfectly is often more understanding than one who sees technical compliance that misses the point.

### Risk-Based Supervision

Regulatory exams focus on **risk**. A low-risk deficiency receives different treatment than a high-risk one.

The SEC's examination priorities explicitly focus on areas of **higher risk** to investors and markets.[^2]

*Source: SEC Office of Examinations[^2]*

## What "Substantially Implemented" Actually Looks Like

### Scenario 1: Systems Not Fully Tested

**The situation:** Your new transaction monitoring system is live, but you haven't completed the final validation testing.

**Is it substantially implemented?**

Possibly. Regulators understand that implementation is a process. If:
- The system is operational and catching violations
- Testing is ongoing and on schedule
- Management is aware and monitoring progress

Then you likely have substantial implementation.

**What to document:**
- Test plan and timeline
- Results to date
- Management sign-off on go-live decision
- Ongoing monitoring protocols

### Scenario 2: Written Policies Lag Behind Practice

**The situation:** Your team is following new procedures, but the written policies haven't been updated.

**Is it substantially implemented?**

Probably not. Written policies serve several purposes:
- They communicate expectations to staff
- They create accountability
- They show regulators what you intended to do

If you can't show what you were trying to do, it's harder to demonstrate you did it.

**What to document:**
- Draft policies in progress
- Communication to staff about procedures
- Training records

### Scenario 3: Vendor Systems Not Ready

**The situation:** Your compliance vendor hasn't delivered a required feature on schedule.

**Is it substantially implemented?**

Maybe. If:
- You've selected and contracted with a vendor
- The feature is on the vendor's roadmap
- You've implemented compensating controls in the meantime

**What to document:**
- Vendor contract and timeline
- Alternative controls in place
- Regular check-ins with vendor

## What "Substantially Implemented" Doesn't Mean

### It's Not an Excuse for Inaction

If you haven't started compliance work, "substantially implemented" won't save you. Regulators distinguish between:
- **Active implementation:** Working toward compliance in good faith
- **Passive non-compliance:** Waiting to see what happens

### It's Not a Safe Harbor

Even substantial compliance doesn't protect you from enforcement if problems occur. If you're 80% compliant and an 80%-covered violation happens, you may still face action.

### It's Not Uniform

What counts as "substantial" varies by:
- **Rule:** Some rules are binary (you either file the form or you don't). Others are graduated.
- **Regulator:** Some agencies are more flexible than others.
- **Risk:** Higher-risk situations require higher compliance standards.

## Internal Controls: The Foundation

Regulators look for evidence that compliance isn't just a checklist — it's a **system**.

The GAO's Standards for Internal Control (the "Green Book") outlines five components:

1. **Control Environment** — Tone at the top, accountability
2. **Risk Assessment** — Identifying and evaluating risks
3. **Control Activities** — Policies and procedures
4. **Information and Communication** — Getting the right information to the right people
5. **Monitoring** — Ongoing evaluation of controls[^3]

*Source: GAO, "Standards for Internal Control in the Federal Government" (2014)[^3]*

---

## The OCC's Framework

The Comptroller of the Currency's Handbook describes internal control evaluation:

> "Examiners evaluate the quality of internal control through:
> - Inquiry and discussion
> - Observation of operations
> - Inspection of documents and records
> - Reperformance of control procedures"[^4]

This is how examiners assess whether your compliance is real — not just documented.

*Source: OCC, "Internal Control" Comptroller's Handbook (2001, revised 2025)[^4]*

## How to Demonstrate Substantial Implementation

### 1. Document Everything

Regulators can't give credit for work they can't see. Maintain:
- Implementation timelines and milestones
- Progress reports
- Decisions made and why
- Issues identified and how they were addressed

### 2. Show Management Oversight

Board and management involvement is a key indicator of substantive compliance:
- Board minutes showing compliance discussed
- Management presentations on implementation
- Escalation procedures for issues

### 3. Implement Compensating Controls

If you can't fully implement a control, show what you're doing instead:
- Why the alternative achieves the same purpose
- How it reduces the identified risk
- When the primary control will be in place

### 4. Self-Report Proactively

If you know you're not fully compliant:
- Tell the regulator
- Explain your timeline
- Show your plan

Self-reporting demonstrates good faith and gives you some control over the narrative.

### 5. Get Legal Advice

If you're uncertain whether your implementation is sufficient, consult outside counsel. An opinion from counsel — even if wrong — shows you took the question seriously.

## The FINRA Framework

FINRA's compliance review process provides a useful model:

1. **Pre-examination:** Firms receive an examination scope letter identifying areas to review
2. **Document request:** Firms submit materials demonstrating compliance
3. **Interviews:** Staff speak with compliance and business personnel
4. **Findings:** Deficiencies identified and discussed
5. **Response:** Firm has opportunity to respond before findings are finalized
6. **Follow-up:** Required remediation tracked[^5]

*Source: FINRA[^5]*

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Substantial compliance is a real standard** — you don't need to be perfect
2. **It requires good faith effort** — regulators can tell the difference between active implementation and passive non-compliance
3. **Documentation is essential** — if you can't show it, it didn't happen
4. **Compensating controls help** — when primary controls aren't ready, alternatives demonstrate risk management
5. **Self-reporting demonstrates good faith** — and gives you some control over the outcome
6. **Get legal advice when uncertain** — an ounce of counsel prevents a pound of enforcement
