# The Lifecycle of a Regulation

*How a rule goes from idea to enforcement*

---

When you read "the SEC issued a new rule," what actually happened? Most people picture a light switch — one day it's off, the next it's on. The reality is a multi-year process with dozens of steps, multiple public comment periods, and plenty of room for delays.

Understanding this lifecycle is essential for anyone who needs to comply with regulations — or anyone who wants to influence them.

---

## The Five Stages of a Regulation

### Stage 1: The Statutory Foundation

Congress passes a law that delegates authority to a federal agency. This is called an **enabling statute**.

For example, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 gave the SEC authority to regulate securities markets. When Congress wants the SEC to address a new issue (like crypto), it either amends existing law or passes new legislation that tells the SEC what to do.

The statute typically specifies:
- **What** the agency must regulate
- **Who** has authority (which agency)
- **General deadlines** (sometimes)
- **What powers** the agency has

*Source: King & Spalding, "A Brief Overview of the Federal Rulemaking Process in the United States" (2024)*[^1]

---

### Stage 2: Proposed Rule (NPRM)

The agency publishes a **Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM)** in the Federal Register.

The notice must include:
- A statement of the time, place, and nature of proceedings
- Reference to the legal authority for the rule
- The terms or substance of the proposed rule (or a description of the subjects involved)

This is where the public gets involved. The Administrative Procedure Act (APA) requires agencies to give "interested persons an opportunity to participate in the rule making through submission of written data, views, or arguments."[^2]

**Typical timeline:** The comment period is usually 30-60 days, but can be extended.[^3]

*Source: Administrative Conference of the United States (ACUS), "Notice-and-Comment Rulemaking" (2021)*[^2]

---

### Stage 3: Comment Period and Final Rule

After reviewing comments, the agency may:
- Adopt the rule as proposed
- Modify the rule based on comments
- Withdraw the rule entirely

The agency must respond to significant comments in the preamble to the final rule. This is where you can see what changed and why.

**The Paperwork Reduction Act** requires agencies to estimate the burden on businesses and get OMB approval for collections of information.[^1]

**The Congressional Review Act** allows Congress to overturn rules before they take effect, though this requires presidential signature.

*Source: National Archives, Administrative Procedure Act, 5 U.S.C. § 553[^4]; King & Spalding[^1]*

---

### Stage 4: Effective Date and Compliance Date

These are **not the same thing**.

- **Effective date:** When the rule becomes law (published in the Federal Register)
- **Compliance date:** When regulated entities must actually follow the rule

Regulators typically give businesses 12-24 months to come into compliance with new rules. This is called a **phased implementation**.

The GENIUS Act, signed in December 2025, gave issuers until June 2026 to comply — roughly 18 months from passage.[^5]

**Why phased?** Companies need time to:
- Update systems and processes
- Train staff
- Test new compliance procedures
- Coordinate with vendors and partners

*Source: See our follow-up post: "Phased Implementation: Why Rules Don't Flip a Switch"*

---

### Stage 5: Enforcement

Once a rule is effective, the agency begins enforcement.

Enforcement happens through:
- **Examinations:** Agency staff review your compliance (like SEC or FINRA exams)
- **Self-reporting:** Companies are often required to report their own violations
- **Whistleblowers:** Tip lines and reward programs
- **Investigations:** Formal inquiries into specific conduct

The enforcement stack ranges from warnings to consent orders to fines. We'll cover this in detail in a future post.

*Source: Morgan Lewis, "SEC and FINRA Enforcement Trends for Broker-Dealers: 2026 Outlook" (2026)*[^6]

---

## How Long Does This Actually Take?

| Stage | Typical Duration |
|-------|------------------|
| Congressional action | 6 months - 2+ years |
| Agency rulemaking | 1-3 years |
| Comment period | 30-90 days |
| Compliance ramp-up | 12-24 months |
| **Total (typical)** | **3-7 years** |

The SEC's rule on cryptocurrency custody took years of deliberation, multiple proposals, and extensive industry comment before reaching final form.[^7]

---

## Why This Matters

**For compliance teams:** Knowing which stage a regulation is in tells you how much time you have. A proposed rule isn't urgent — a final rule with a compliance date 6 months out is.

**For advocates:** The comment period is your moment to shape the rule. Once it's final, changing it requires either new legislation or a lengthy rulemaking process.

**For everyone else:** "The government passed a rule" is just the beginning of a years-long process. When you see headlines about a new regulation, ask: which stage is it actually in?

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Regulations don't appear overnight — they go through a formal, public process
2. The comment period is your opportunity to influence the final rule
3. Effective date ≠ compliance date — you get time to implement
4. Enforcement comes last, after months or years of lead time
5. Understanding the stage tells you what actions to take — and when
