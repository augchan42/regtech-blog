# The Enforcement Stack

*From exam to fine: how regulators actually enforce rules*

---

Most people think of enforcement as a courtroom drama — regulators catching a bad actor and punishing them publicly. The reality is quieter, slower, and more nuanced.

This post explains the full enforcement process — from routine examinations to multi-million dollar fines — and what compliance teams need to know to navigate it.

---

## The Enforcement Stack: A Hierarchy

Enforcement isn't a single event. It's a **stack** of escalating interventions, each with different implications.

| Level | Action | Typical Trigger |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| 1 | Routine Examination | Scheduled review |
| 2 | Deficiency Letter | Minor findings |
| 3 | Special Examination / Sweep | Specific concern or industry-wide review |
| 4 | Matter Requiring Attention (MRA) / Under Review | Significant compliance gaps |
| 5 | Investigation | Evidence of potential violation |
| 6 | Wells Notice | Regulators plan to bring action |
| 7 | Consent Order / Settlement | Negotiated resolution |
| 8 | Administrative Proceeding / Trial | Unresolved disputes |
| 9 | Fine / Sanction | Final outcome |
| 10 | License Revocation / Ban | Most severe outcome |

---

## Level 1: The Routine Examination

### What It Is

Regulatory examination is the primary way agencies monitor compliance. Unlike criminal investigations, exams are generally non-adversarial — the goal is to identify and correct problems before they become violations.

### Who Examines

| Agency | What They Examine |
|--------|------------------|
| SEC | Securities brokers, investment advisers, exchanges |
| FINRA | Broker-dealers, registered representatives |
| OCC / FDIC / Federal Reserve | Banks |
| FinCEN | Money services businesses (including crypto exchanges) |
| FCA (UK) | UK-regulated financial firms |
| ESMA / National regulators (EU) | MiCA compliance, AML |

### How It Works

The SEC uses a **risk-focused examination** approach. Staff prioritize areas of highest risk based on:
- The firm's business model
- Prior examination findings
- Market events and trends
- Tips and complaints[^1]

FINRA examines broker-dealers on a cycle — typically every 2-4 years for smaller firms, more frequently for larger ones.[^2]

*Source: SEC Office of Examinations[^1]; Morgan Lewis, "SEC and FINRA Enforcement Trends for Broker-Dealers" (2026)[^2]*

## Level 2-4: Deficiency Letters, MRAs, and Sweeps

### Deficiency Letters

After an examination, the agency sends a **deficiency letter** identifying specific problems. These are generally:
- Confidential
- Not public record
- Expected to be corrected within a specified timeframe

A deficiency letter is not an enforcement action — it's a chance to fix the problem. Most firms take these seriously.

### Matters Requiring Attention (MRAs)

An MRA (used by the SEC) or similar designation is more serious than a deficiency. It indicates:
- Significant compliance gaps
- Problems that need board-level attention
- Issues that, if uncorrected, could lead to formal action

### Industry Sweeps

Sometimes regulators conduct **sweeps** — examining multiple firms simultaneously on the same issue. This happens when:
- A new rule has been implemented
- A new risk has emerged across the industry
- One firm's problem suggests a broader pattern

In 2025-2026, regulators conducted sweeps focused on AI in compliance, stablecoin reserve requirements, and crypto custody practices.[^2]

*Source: Morgan Lewis[^2]*

## Level 5-6: Investigations and Wells Notices

### Investigations

When examination findings suggest potential violations, the matter may be referred to the **Division of Enforcement**. An investigation involves:
- Formal document requests (subpoenas)
- Sworn testimony
- Evidence gathering
- Interviews with witnesses

Investigations are confidential. The subject of an investigation usually doesn't know it's happening until they receive a **Wells Notice**.

### Wells Notice

The Wells Notice (named after the SEC's 1972 Wells Committee) tells a subject that the staff intends to recommend enforcement action to the Commission.

Recipients have an opportunity to:
- Submit a written statement (the "Wells Submission")
- Argue why charges shouldn't be filed
- Present mitigating factors

The Wells process gives subjects a chance to influence the decision before charges are formally filed.[^2]

*Source: Morgan Lewis[^2]*

## Level 7-9: Settlements and Fines

### Consent Orders

Most enforcement actions are resolved through **consent orders** — negotiated settlements where the subject neither admits nor denies the allegations.

A consent order typically includes:
- **Censure / reprimand**
- **Fine** (often calculated based on profits made or harm caused)
- **Disgorgement** (returning ill-gotten gains)
- **Compliance requirements** (new policies, audits, monitoring)
- **Self-reporting obligations**

### How Fines Are Calculated

FINRA and the SEC use different approaches, but both consider:
- **Severity of the violation**
- **Duration** (how long did it continue?)
- **Volume** (how many transactions affected?)
- **Awareness** (did the firm know and correct it?)
- **Self-reporting** (did the firm report itself?)
- **Cooperation** (did the firm help the investigation?)

The 2025 FINRA Sanctions Study found that fines vary widely — from thousands to tens of millions of dollars. The largest fine in 2025 was $26 million; in 2024, it was $6 million.[^3]

*Source: Eversheds Sutherland, "2025 FINRA Sanctions Study" (2026)[^3]*

## The AML Enforcement Case Study

Anti-money laundering (AML) violations consistently generate some of the largest fines.

### Top Enforcement Issues by Fine (2025)

Based on FINRA's 2025 Sanctions Study:

1. **Anti-Money Laundering** — highest total fines
2. **Misleading Communications**
3. **Trade Reporting**
4. **Recordkeeping**
5. **Regulation Best Interest**[^3]

This reflects regulators' continued focus on financial crime compliance — especially in the crypto sector, where OFAC has taken aggressive action.

**Example:** Exodus Movement agreed to pay $3.1 million to settle OFAC sanctions violations. The company — a non-custodial wallet provider — facilitated transactions by users in sanctioned jurisdictions through third-party services integrated into its platform.[^4]

*Source: DLA Piper, "OFAC sanctions enforcement in fintech and crypto" (2026)[^4]*

## Self-Reporting: The Double-Edged Sword

Many regulations require firms to **self-report** violations. The decision to self-report is one of the most consequential in compliance.

### Benefits of Self-Reporting
- Demonstrates good faith
- Often results in reduced penalties
- Shows regulators you're a cooperative compliance partner

### Risks of Self-Reporting
- Triggers formal investigation
- Creates liability exposure
- May not result in leniency if the violation is severe

The DOJ's corporate compliance guidance makes clear that self-reporting is a factor — but not a guarantee of leniency. Cooperation, remediation, and the nature of the violation all matter.[^2]

*Source: Morgan Lewis[^2]*

## What Regulators Actually Look For

Based on examination guidance and enforcement actions, regulators focus on:

### 1. Governance

- Is compliance a board-level priority?
- Are there clear policies and procedures?
- Does management take compliance seriously?

### 2. Risk Assessment

- Has the firm identified its compliance risks?
- Are high-risk areas receiving appropriate attention?
- Is the risk assessment updated as conditions change?

### 3. Testing and Monitoring

- Are compliance programs tested regularly?
- Are issues identified and remediated promptly?
- Is there a culture of continuous improvement?

### 4. Training

- Do employees understand their compliance obligations?
- Is training updated for new products and risks?
- Are there consequences for compliance failures?

*Source: Federal Reserve SR 96-14, revised October 2025[^5]; OCC Comptroller's Handbook[^6]*

## Cryptocurrency-Specific Enforcement Trends

### 2025-2026 Enforcement Priorities

1. **Stablecoin reserve requirements** — Are issuers maintaining 1:1 backing?
2. **Custody practices** — Are assets properly segregated and secured?
3. **Travel Rule compliance** — Are VASPs exchanging originator/beneficiary information?
4. **AI in compliance** — Are AI systems explainable and auditable?
5. **Retail investor protection** — Are crypto marketing materials fair and balanced?

The SEC's March 2026 interpretive release on crypto assets marks a shift toward clearer rules — and likely increased enforcement as ambiguity decreases.[^7]

*Source: SEC Release 33-11412, March 17, 2026[^7]*

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Enforcement is a stack, not a switch** — most issues are caught and corrected before becoming formal actions
2. **Examinations are the front line** — routine exams find most problems
3. **Self-reporting matters** — but it doesn't guarantee leniency
4. **Fines vary widely** — from thousands to hundreds of millions
5. **Cooperation helps** — regulators remember who made their job easier
6. **Culture counts** — regulators look at whether compliance is real, not just documented
