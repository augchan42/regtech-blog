# Reading a Regulation: A Field Guide

*How to navigate the CFR without losing your mind*

---

The Code of Federal Regulations (CFR) contains over 200,000 pages of rules. Reading it from cover to cover would take decades. Learning to read it strategically takes hours.

This guide teaches you how regulations are organized, how to find what you need, and how to decode the legal language that makes compliance feel like a foreign language.

---

## How the CFR Is Organized

The CFR is divided into **50 titles** by subject matter. Each title is divided into **parts**, each part into **sections**.[^1]

**Example:** 17 C.F.R. § 240.10b-5

- **17** = Title 17 (Commodity and Securities Exchanges)
- **240** = Part 240 (Securities Exchange Act of 1934 — General Rules and Regulations)
- **.10b-5** = Section 10b-5 (the classic anti-fraud rule)

*Source: Library of Congress, "Code of Federal Regulations"[^1]*

## Where to Find Regulations

### Official Sources

1. **GovInfo.gov** — The official, certified version of the CFR. Updated annually.[^2]
2. **eCFR.gov** — An unofficial, daily-updated HTML version. Easier to navigate but not legally authoritative.[^1]
3. **Agency websites** — The SEC, FINRA, and other agencies publish their rules on their own websites with helpful annotations.

### Unofficial Sources

1. **Westlaw / LexisNexis** — Annotated versions with case law and secondary sources
2. **Bloomberg Law** — Searchable with practical guidance
3. **Justia / Casetext** — Free but less authoritative

**For compliance purposes:** Always use the official GovInfo version when precision matters. Use eCFR for quick reference.

## How to Read a Regulation: Section by Section

Most regulatory sections follow a similar structure:

### 1. Scope and Application

The first paragraph usually tells you **who** the rule applies to and **what** it covers.

> "This section applies to every member of FINRA and to all securities transactions executed through a member."

### 2. Definitions

Rules define their key terms. Look for a "Definitions" section — often § .1 or § .800.

> "For purposes of this section, the term 'digital asset' means any asset that is issued or transferred using distributed ledger or blockchain technology."

### 3. Requirements

The meat of the rule — what you must do.

> "Members shall establish and maintain a written supervisory system reasonably designed to achieve compliance with applicable securities laws and FINRA rules."

### 4. Exceptions and Exemptions

Carve-outs from the general requirements.

> "This section shall not apply to transactions in digital assets that are not securities, as defined in paragraph (a)(1) of this section."

### 5. Recordkeeping

How long to keep records and in what format.

> "Members shall retain all records required under this section for a period of not less than six years."

### 6. Penalties

What happens if you don't comply. Often references to broader enforcement provisions.

## Decoding Regulatory Language

Regulations use specific words with specific legal meanings.

### "Shall" vs. "May"

- **"Shall"** = mandatory obligation
- **"May"** = permissive, discretionary

> "The member **shall** file a report within 10 days." = Required

> "The member **may** request an extension." = Optional

### "Including" vs. "Including but not limited to"

- **"Including"** = examples are exhaustive (debated, but often interpreted this way)
- **"Including but not limited to"** = examples are non-exhaustive

### "Reasonable" / "Reasonably"

This appears constantly and is intentionally flexible. What is "reasonable" depends on context, and regulators will tell you if your interpretation wasn't reasonable.

> "A supervisory system **reasonably designed** to achieve compliance..."

### "Timely"

No specific time period defined — regulators expect you to know what's appropriate. "Timely" usually means "without unreasonable delay."

## Understanding Regulatory Citations

### Bluebook Format

The standard legal citation style:

> 17 C.F.R. § 240.10b-5 (2025)

This means: Title 17, Part 240, Section 10b-5, 2025 edition of the CFR.

### How to Read a Federal Register Citation

> 89 Fed. Reg. 12,456 (Feb. 28, 2024)

This means: Volume 89 of the Federal Register, starting on page 12,456, published February 28, 2024.

### Cross-References

Regulations constantly cross-reference each other:

> "As defined in § 240.10b-5..." means look at that section for the definition.

## The Preamble: Don't Ignore It

The text of the regulation is only half the story. The **preamble** — the introduction and commentary published with the final rule — contains:

### 1. Summary of the Rule

A plain-English explanation of what the rule does.

### 2. Response to Comments

What the agency said about the comments it received. This is crucial — if you commented on an issue and the agency addressed it (or ignored it), the preamble tells you.

### 3. Economic Analysis

The agency's analysis of the rule's costs and benefits. This is often contested in court.

### 4. Effective and Compliance Dates

When the rule takes effect and when you must comply.

## Finding Crypto-Specific Rules

### SEC Rules (17 C.F.R.)

The SEC has issued guidance and rules on digital assets, including:
- Custody of digital asset securities
- Broker-dealer obligations for crypto
- Securities offering rules for tokenized assets

Key SEC guidance: Release No. 33-11412, "Application of the Federal Securities Laws to Certain Types of Crypto Assets" (March 2026)[^3]

### FinCEN Rules (31 C.F.R.)

FinCEN regulates money services businesses (MSBs), which include many crypto exchanges:
- AML program requirements
- Travel Rule obligations
- Suspicious Activity Report (SAR) filing

### OFAC Sanctions (31 C.F.R. Parts 501-589)

All U.S. persons — including crypto businesses — must comply with OFAC sanctions. The October 2025 sanctions against the Prince Group marked the largest crypto-related sanctions action in history.[^4]

*Source: Elliptic, "Five steps to effective cryptocurrency sanctions compliance in 2026" (2026)[^4]*

## Practical Tips

### 1. Start with the Table of Contents

Most CFR titles have a TOC. Find the right part before diving into sections.

### 2. Use the Index

The CFR has a subject index in each title volume. It's older and less complete than web search, but useful for obscure topics.

### 3. Search by Keyword

eCFR.gov has powerful search. Try searching "digital asset" or "cryptocurrency" across all titles.

### 4. Check for Pending Changes

The Federal Register publishes "Rules Pending" and "Proposed Rules" daily. A section might look current but have amendments in the pipeline.

### 5. Read the Agency's FAQ

Many agencies publish FAQs that explain rules in plain English. These aren't legally binding, but they tell you how the agency interprets the rules.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **The CFR is organized by title → part → section** — learn the key titles for your industry
2. **Use eCFR for navigation, GovInfo for authority** — know the difference
3. **The preamble contains critical context** — read it before assuming you understand the rule
4. **"Shall" is mandatory, "May" is optional** — word choice matters legally
5. **Definitions are usually in § .1** — always start there
6. **Definitions section is always § .1** — always start there
