# Notice and Comment: Your Voice in Rulemaking

*The public's power to shape the rules that govern them*

---

Every major regulation that affects your business started with a notice in the Federal Register asking for public comments. Most people ignore these. That's a mistake — and sometimes a costly one.

The notice-and-comment process under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) is one of the few moments where regulated entities can actually influence how a rule is written, before it becomes law.[^1]

This post explains how the process works, why it matters, and how to submit a comment that gets read.

---

## The Legal Basis

The APA requires federal agencies to publish a notice of proposed rulemaking (NPRM) and give "interested persons an opportunity to participate in the rule making through submission of written data, views, or arguments with or without opportunity for oral presentation."[^2]

This applies to rules with "general applicability and legal effect" — essentially, rules that affect the public broadly.

Key points:
- Agencies **must** publish the notice in the Federal Register
- The notice must include the **legal authority** and the **substance** of the proposed rule
- After the comment period closes, the agency must consider the comments before issuing a final rule[^1]

*Source: Administrative Conference of the United States (ACUS)[^1]; National Archives[^2]*

---

## What the Federal Register Actually Is

The Federal Register is the daily newspaper of the U.S. government. Every proposed rule, final rule, and agency announcement gets published there.

You can find it at [federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov). Each document has:
- A **document number** (e.g., "Release No. 34-105020")
- The **date of publication**
- The **comment deadline**
- Instructions for submitting comments

The SEC's recent crypto framework interpretation, released March 17, 2026, was published in the Federal Register with a formal document number and comment procedures.[^3]

*Source: Federal Register, "Application of the Federal Securities Laws to Certain Types of Crypto Assets," S7-2026-09, March 2026[^3]*

---

## The Comment Period: Your Window

When an NPRM is published, you typically have **30 to 60 days** to comment. The SEC often extends this for complex rules — the 2024 crypto reporting rules had extended comment periods.[^4]

**Critical point:** The comment period is not a suggestion. It is the legally mandated opportunity to influence the rule. Once the comment period closes, changing the rule requires starting the process over.

*Source: SEC, "Engaging in the SEC Rulemaking Process" (2025)[^4]*

---

## Who Can Comment?

Anyone. The APA says "interested persons" — this includes:
- Businesses (large and small)
- Industry associations
- Consumer advocacy groups
- Individual citizens
- Academics and researchers
- Foreign entities affected by U.S. rules

The SEC specifically notes: "A comment does not need to be lengthy or address every item in a proposed rule. Even a couple sentences explaining how a proposed rule would affect you, your company, or your investment decision can be very helpful."[^4]

*Source: SEC[^4]*

---

## What Makes a Good Comment?

### 1. Be Specific

Don't just say "we oppose this rule." Explain:
- What specific provisions you oppose
- Why they would cause harm
- What evidence you have

### 2. Provide Data

Agencies respond to evidence, not just opinions. Include:
- Cost estimates for compliance
- Examples from your operations
- Market data that contradicts agency assumptions

### 3. Propose Alternatives

If you oppose something, offer a better approach. Agencies are more likely to respond to "this provision will cause X problem; a better approach would be Y" than simply "don't do this."

### 4. Include Technical Expertise

If you're commenting on a technical rule (like crypto custody requirements), include engineers or compliance specialists who can speak to the technical realities. The SEC has specific guidance on what to include.[^4]

### 5. Watch for "Reply Comments"

After the initial comment period, agencies sometimes open a **reply comment period** for responses to the initial comments. This is your chance to rebut industry arguments or agency responses.

---

## Do Comments Actually Matter?

Yes — but not always in the way you expect.

Agencies are required to consider all comments, but they don't have to do what commenters want. What they *must* do is respond to significant comments in the preamble to the final rule.

This creates a paper trail. If an agency ignores a major concern raised by multiple commenters, that can become grounds for a legal challenge later.

**Recent example:** The Treasury and IRS received over 29,000 comments on the hydrogen production tax credit regulations under the Inflation Reduction Act — an unprecedented response that forced extended deliberation.[^5]

*Source: King & Spalding, "A Brief Overview of the Federal Rulemaking Process" (2024)[^5]*

---

## How to Submit a Comment

### For Most Federal Agencies:
1. Go to [regulations.gov](https://www.regsiter.gov)
2. Search for the rule by name or document number
3. Click "Comment Now"
4. Fill in your information and submit

### For the SEC:
You have three options:
- **Online:** SEC's comment form on SEC.gov
- **Email:** rule-comments@sec.gov
- **Mail:** Secretary, Securities and Exchange Commission, 100 F Street NE, Washington DC 20549-1090[^4]

**Pro tip:** Include the file number (found on the first page of the proposal) in your submission.

### For the CFPB, FDIC, OCC, and Federal Reserve:
Most use the same regulations.gov portal, though some have separate processes.

---

## What Happens to Your Comment?

1. Your comment is added to the public docket
2. Agency staff read and categorize comments
3. The agency summarizes key themes in the final rule preamble
4. Significant comments get specific responses
5. The final rule is published with a "Response to Comments" section

**Your comment becomes part of the public record.** Anyone can read it. This includes:
- Future regulators reviewing the rule's history
- Lawyers challenging the rule in court
- Journalists researching the rule's development

*Source: ACUS[^1]*

## Key Takeaways

1. **The comment period is your best chance to shape a rule** — before it becomes enforceable
2. **You don't need to be a lawyer or large corporation** — individual comments are welcome
3. **Quality matters more than quantity** — a well-reasoned, data-backed comment beats thousands of form letters
4. **Submit early and often** — the window closes, and reply comments are rare
5. **Your comment becomes public record** — it can influence future interpretation of the rule
